Currently the Trail exists in an appealingly raw and unmanaged state, reflecting the passing of the railroad era as it inexorably reverts to a natural environment in the post-industrial landscape. There are miles of empty walls, abandoned trestles and overpasses, iron bridges and industrial detritus. Along the way are graffiti tags, broken bottles, metal artifacts, and indefatigable nature, reclaiming it all. Using digital tools and industrial materials along with photography, painting, drawing and collage, my work explores the interface of nature and technology. Variations in scale reveal recursive patterns, referencing visible structures in nature, macroscopic or microscopic views, or complex network visualization in topography and computing. I seek a common language: order and complexity, the organic and the built, the rational and the intuitive, the mark of the hand and the mark of the machine. Reflecting my interest in specific local environments, I photograph on-site and render the images into abstract forms, pairing and integrating the human/industrial detritus with corresponding forms from nature. I am interested in the primacy of color, pattern and visual complexity in connecting the viewer to the environment. These images are proposals for public art installations along the Bloomingdale Trail. They are intended to bring attention to the unique qualities of this urban space, and to engage the public in helping to move the park project forward. As of now they are unfunded. I hope that public exposure may lead to their realization.
